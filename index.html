Introduction

The purpose of this experiment is to study optical properties of various sources, such as computer displays and light emitting plugins. The study is for an upper-level undergraduate physics course on optics. This research is based on the first module of the course. It is designed to familizarize us with the tools necessary for measuring and describing optical properties. The culmination of this research is based on two parts. Part one is the study of a computer display. Using a Topcon SR-3 Photoradiometer, we characterized a display based on professional settings. Settings include, spectral radiance, luminance, color temperature, contrast ratio, and color quality based on the 1931 and 1976 CIE Systems. By analyzing these settings, we were able to understand performance standards for a LCD computer display.
Part two of the research measures optical properties of different light sources. We studied three types of light sources: a calibrated light source, a 10W incandescent light bulb, and a green light emitting diode (LED). We measured similar optical properties as the first part of the module. The purpose was to use the tools of the previous part to observe the radiance and luminance of different light sources through opal glass.

Abstract

In this experiment we measured the optical properties of an LCD Display, incandescent light bulb, and a LED. For the LCD, white and black measurements matched industry standards with white having a luminance of 173 nits at a correlated color temperature (CCT) of 7600K. The contrast ratio between white and black came out to be 414:1 which is higher than the industry standard of 300:1. Color coordinates were accurate and matched the regions of the 1931 CIE coordinate system. The value of our gamma curve was found to be 2.07 which is lower than the industry standard. For the light bulb, we found the luminous flux to be 170 lm with an efficacy of 171 lm/W at a maximum voltage of 12V. CCT was first found to be 1579K at 3V, while at 12V it was 2549K. For the LED, luminous flux was measured to be 0.0569 lm with a CCT of 7236K when placed 30 cm away from an integrating sphere that had a variable diaphragm set at a max radius of 25 mm.

Module One - Part One

We began our experiment with part one, the characterization of an LCD display. Before taking any measurements, it was important to make sure our display's surface vector was normal to the photradiometer. To do this, we placed a laser above the Topcon instrument, overlayed the LCD display with a mirror, then adjusted the height of the photoradiometer and laser with respect to its reflection from the mirror. We did this until the laser light reflected back upon itself as shown below:


Once the Topcon's sight was aligned with the normal vector, we powered on the device and looked through its lens. Optical properties of the LCD screen were then measured at different pure colors of the rgb spectrum.

The following image represents the red light's measured spectral radiance as a function of wavelength:

The lower left graph indicates measurments of pure rgb colors to be in their correct positions based on the 1931 color coordinate system, with white being the graph's standard near the center. Next, we recorded data of the screen's gray scale properties, stepping rgb values by 16.

In this step of the experiment, the LCD was set to output an rgb value of (255, 255, 255), outputing white light. The rgb scale was then decremented from 255 to 0. With each change of the display, spectral radiance was recorded. White and black light from the display was measured twice. This was done to mitigate any possible errors. By collecting the same points twice, we can compare the results for any discrepencies.

Analysis

Characteristics of the LCD display were analyzed using Excel. With data from the photoradiometer, we observed luminance and color temperature of the white images, contrast ratio between white and black, quality of the colors compared to the 1931 and 1976 CIE standards, and the gamma curve. Module One - Part Two

In the second part of the experiment, we measured similar optical properties of different light sources. Those sources include a calibrated light source, a 10W incandescent light bulb, and a green LED. In order to control the conical direction of light from the sources, each light emitting source was mounted inside an integrating sphere. A small spherical shell used to contain and direct light sources through opal glass. The following images show the 10W light bulb before and after installation into the sphere:

Before turning on the 10W light bulb, baseline data was recorded with the calibrated light source through the opal glass. This establishes a standard set of values across the visible light spectrum from 380nm to 780nm. We are able to compare data of other light sources to the calibrated results.
